# Results Summary

The main adjusted models estimated percent differences in pollutant concentrations for IDES households compared with traditional cookstove households.

## Main Finding

IDES use was associated with lower pollutant concentrations, especially during cooking-period windows.

| Outcome | Adjusted percent difference for IDES vs traditional |
| --- | ---: |
| PM10, cooking period | -28% |
| PM2.5, cooking period | -36% |
| PM1, cooking period | -38% |
| Black carbon, cooking period | -43% |
| Carbon monoxide, cooking period | -17% |
| PM10, 24-hour | about -18% |
| PM2.5, 24-hour | about -18% |
| PM1, 24-hour | about -20% |

## Interpretation

The strongest associations appeared during cooking periods, which supports the interpretation that improved cookstove use was most closely linked to lower direct cooking-related emissions. Smaller 24-hour differences suggest that background sources, ventilation, stove stacking, and non-cooking household activities also contribute to total daily exposure.

## Other Determinants

Indoor air quality was also associated with:

- electricity access
- focus vs regular village-program group
- kitchen location
- household size
- roof material

These findings suggest that cleaner cooking technology may be most effective when paired with maintenance support, consistent use, improved household infrastructure, and reduced reliance on other combustion sources.
